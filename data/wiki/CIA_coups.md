# CIA Coups

## Introduction
The Central Intelligence Agency <a href="/documents/CIA" class="citation" target="_blank">[CIA]</a> has been involved in numerous covert operations, including orchestrating coups, since its inception in 1947. These operations have often aimed to counter perceived threats from communism, influence foreign governments, and secure U.S. interests abroad <a href="/pdf/CIA-comments_1994-2005u#page=162" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1994-2005u, p.162)</a>. This article provides a comprehensive overview of notable CIA-backed coups, examining their historical contexts, motivations, outcomes, and the broader implications for U.S. foreign policy. The CIA's involvement in these operations has been a subject of considerable debate and analysis, reflecting the complexities of international relations during the Cold War and beyond <a href="/pdf/DIAintelLawHndbk_2004#page=118" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2004, p.118)</a>.

## Historical Context
The CIA's involvement in coups can be traced back to the early years of the Cold War, a period characterized by ideological conflict between the United States and the Soviet Union. The U.S. government viewed the spread of communism as a significant threat, prompting interventions in various nations to install pro-American governments <a href="/pdf/CIA-comments_1994-2005u#page=162" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1994-2005u, p.162)</a> <a href="/pdf/ODNIcovertOps1800s2007#page=14" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ODNIcovertOps1800s2007, p.14)</a>. The CIA employed a variety of tactics, including propaganda, political manipulation, and direct support for military actions <a href="/pdf/NSA-AmerCryptColdWarBk1#page=105" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #NSA-AmerCryptColdWarBk1, p.105)</a>.

### Cold War Era <a href="/documents/1947-1991" class="citation" target="_blank">[1947-1991]</a>
During the Cold War, the CIA executed several high-profile coups in response to the rise of socialist and communist movements in various countries. This period saw a significant increase in covert operations as the agency sought to contain Soviet influence globally <a href="/pdf/NSA-AmerCryptColdWarBk1#page=105" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #NSA-AmerCryptColdWarBk1, p.105)</a>. The actions taken by the CIA during this time were often justified as necessary measures to protect U.S. interests and promote stability in regions considered vital to American geopolitical strategy <a href="/pdf/DIAintelLawHndbk_2004#page=118" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2004, p.118)</a>.

## Notable CIA Coups

### Iran <a href="/documents/1953" class="citation" target="_blank">[1953]</a>
In 1953, the CIA orchestrated a coup to overthrow the democratically elected Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh in Iran. Mossadegh had nationalized the Iranian oil industry, prompting concern among Western powers, particularly Britain and the United States <a href="/pdf/CIAhistOSAincep-1969u#page=897" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #CIAhistOSAincep-1969u, p.897)</a>. The operation, known as Operation Ajax, was carried out with the assistance of the British intelligence agency MI6 <a href="/pdf/CIA-comments_1994-2005u#page=162" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1994-2005u, p.162)</a>. The coup resulted in the reinstatement of the Shah, Mohammad Reza Pahlavi, leading to years of authoritarian rule and significant unrest that would culminate in the 1979 Iranian Revolution <a href="/documents/DIA_FileId_238464_chunk12" class="citation" target="_blank">[DIA_FileId_238464_chunk12]</a>.

#### Key Facts:
- **Date:** August 19, 1953
- **Operation Name:** Operation Ajax
- **Outcome:** Restoration of the Shah's monarchy, leading to long-term instability <a href="/documents/DIA_FileId_238464_chunk12" class="citation" target="_blank">[DIA_FileId_238464_chunk12]</a>.

### Guatemala <a href="/documents/1954" class="citation" target="_blank">[1954]</a>
In 1954, the CIA executed a coup in Guatemala, known as Operation PBSUCCESS, to depose President Jacobo Árbenz, who had implemented land reforms that threatened U.S. economic interests <a href="/pdf/ODNIcovertOps1800s2007#page=14" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ODNIcovertOps1800s2007, p.14)</a>. The CIA supported a rebel army led by Carlos Castillo Armas, which successfully overthrew Árbenz and established a military dictatorship. This coup had lasting repercussions, leading to decades of civil conflict in Guatemala <a href="/pdf/DIAintelLawHndbk_2004#page=118" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2004, p.118)</a>.

#### Key Facts:
- **Date:** June 27, 1954
- **Operation Name:** Operation PBSUCCESS
- **Outcome:** Establishment of a military dictatorship, prolonged civil war <a href="/pdf/CIAhistOSAincep-1969u#page=897" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #CIAhistOSAincep-1969u, p.897)</a>.

### Cuba <a href="/documents/1961" class="citation" target="_blank">[1961]</a>
The CIA's most infamous operation was the Bay of Pigs invasion in April 1961, aimed at overthrowing Fidel Castro's communist government in Cuba. The plan involved training and equipping Cuban exiles to invade the island. The operation ended in failure, causing embarrassment for the U.S. government and solidifying Castro's power while pushing Cuba closer to the Soviet Union <a href="/pdf/CIAhistOSAincep-1969u#page=897" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #CIAhistOSAincep-1969u, p.897)</a> <a href="/pdf/DIAintelLawHndbk_2004#page=118" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2004, p.118)</a>.

#### Key Facts:
- **Date:** April 17-19, 1961
- **Operation Name:** Bay of Pigs Invasion
- **Outcome:** Failure and consolidation of Castro's regime <a href="/pdf/DIAintelLawHndbk_2004#page=118" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2004, p.118)</a>.

### Dominican Republic <a href="/documents/1965" class="citation" target="_blank">[1965]</a>
In 1965, the CIA intervened in the Dominican Republic during a civil war following the ousting of President Juan Bosch. The U.S. feared that Bosch's return would lead to a communist government. The CIA supported a military intervention that restored a pro-American regime, but the instability continued for years <a href="/documents/DIA_FileId_238464_chunk12" class="citation" target="_blank">[DIA_FileId_238464_chunk12]</a>.

#### Key Facts:
- **Date:** April 1965
- **Operation Name:** Operation Power Pack
- **Outcome:** Restoration of a military government, continued instability <a href="/pdf/CIAhistOSAincep-1969u#page=897" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #CIAhistOSAincep-1969u, p.897)</a>.

### Chile <a href="/documents/1973" class="citation" target="_blank">[1973]</a>
One of the most consequential coups supported by the CIA occurred in Chile on September 11, 1973. The agency backed General Augusto Pinochet's overthrow of democratically elected President Salvador Allende. The coup was part of a broader strategy to counter leftist movements in Latin America. Pinochet's regime was marked by severe human rights abuses <a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_239146#page=9" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #239146, p.9)</a>.

#### Key Facts:
- **Date:** September 11, 1973
- **Outcome:** Establishment of a military dictatorship, extensive human rights violations <a href="/documents/DIA_FileId_238464_chunk12" class="citation" target="_blank">[DIA_FileId_238464_chunk12]</a>.

### Nicaragua <a href="/documents/1980s" class="citation" target="_blank">[1980s]</a>
In the 1980s, the CIA was involved in supporting anti-Sandinista forces known as the Contras during the Nicaraguan Civil War. This covert operation aimed to undermine the Sandinista government, which had overthrown a U.S.-backed dictator. The U.S. Congress eventually restricted funding for the Contras, leading to the Iran-Contra Affair, which scandalized the Reagan administration <a href="/pdf/CIA-OIGinvsClosed_2007-2011#page=16" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2007-2011, p.16)</a>.

#### Key Facts:
- **Date:** 1980s
- **Outcome:** Prolonged civil conflict, significant political fallout in the U.S. <a href="/documents/DIA_FileId_238464_chunk12" class="citation" target="_blank">[DIA_FileId_238464_chunk12]</a>.

## Implications and Analysis
The legacy of CIA-backed coups extends beyond immediate geopolitical outcomes. These operations often fostered long-term instability, civil wars, and resentment towards U.S. interventionism <a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_239234#page=3" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #239234, p.3)</a>. The methods employed, including disinformation campaigns and support for authoritarian regimes, have generated significant criticism and debate regarding U.S. foreign policy ethics <a href="/pdf/DIAintelLawHndbk_2004#page=118" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2004, p.118)</a>.

### Consequences of CIA Coups
1. **Destabilization**: Many CIA-supported regimes faced backlash leading to civil unrest and prolonged conflicts <a href="/documents/DIA_FileId_238864_chunk12" class="citation" target="_blank">[DIA_FileId_238864_chunk12]</a>.
2. **Human Rights Violations**: Overthrowing governments often resulted in human rights abuses by subsequent regimes, tarnishing the U.S.'s image abroad <a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_239146#page=9" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #239146, p.9)</a>.
3. **Anti-American Sentiment**: Covert interventions have contributed to a legacy of distrust towards the U.S. in various regions, particularly in Latin America and the Middle East <a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_239234#page=3" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #239234, p.3)</a>.
