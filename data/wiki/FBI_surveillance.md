## FBI Surveillance

### Introduction
FBI surveillance refers to the FBI’s collection and analysis of information across electronic and physical surveillance domains. <a href="/pdf/9IC-IGclosDocsInvests_2012-2013#page=9" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2012-2013, p.9)</a><a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=114" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.114)</a> All data from FBI investigations are maintained at FBI Headquarters in the Central Records System and are accessible to authorized personnel under established controls. <a href="/pdf/FBI-AshReport_1977-1981#page=246" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1977-1981, p.246)</a> This centralized data handling is a core feature of the FBI’s information management practices. <a href="/pdf/FBI-AshReport_1977-1981#page=246" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1977-1981, p.246)</a> The modern legal and policy framework for domestic FBI operations has evolved to balance investigative needs with civil liberties, drawing on Attorney General guidelines, statutory authorities, and interagency coordination. <a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=119" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.119)</a><a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=122" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.122)</a>

### Overview of surveillance modalities

#### Electronic surveillance
Electronic surveillance has long been described in declassified materials as a core investigative tool, including electronic eavesdropping and related techniques. <a href="/pdf/9IC-IGclosDocsInvests_2012-2013#page=9" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2012-2013, p.9)</a> FBI guidance emphasizes targeting legally authorized subjects and minimizing intrusion, reflecting a broader professional framework for electronic surveillance practices. <a href="/pdf/FBItransBriefBiden_2020#page=12" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2020, p.12)</a> Surveillance must align with applicable statutes and approvals and protect civil liberties where appropriate. <a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=120" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.120)</a>

#### Physical surveillance
Covert physical surveillance outside overt settings requires coordination with relevant authorities and adherence to applicable rules. <a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=114" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.114)</a> Coordination mechanisms are described as existing to support mission objectives while respecting legal constraints. <a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=93" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.93)</a>

#### Pretexts and covert techniques
Historical FBI operations have employed pretexts and cover techniques to gather information while concealing investigative purposes. <a href="/pdf/FBI_Pretexts_and_Cover_Techniques_May-1956#page=31" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #May-1956, p.31)</a>

#### Unmanned and airborne surveillance
Contemporary materials address the use of unmanned aerial systems (UAS/UAVs) for surveillance, with policy and regulatory structures drawing on the Fourth Amendment, privacy protections, FAA rules, and internal FBI guidelines governing domestic operations. <a href="/pdf/FBIcorresHJC_2012-2013#page=70" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2012-2013, p.70)</a> UAV activities are described as operating within the FBI’s domestic investigations framework and aviation regulations. <a href="/pdf/FBIcorresHJC_2012-2013#page=70" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2012-2013, p.70)</a>

#### Data handling and indexing
FOIA-released documents indicate that sensitive data from surveillance activities are cataloged and indexed within FBI information-management systems. <a href="/pdf/FBI-FOIA-PA-Ref-Manual#page=586" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #FBI-FOIA-PA-Ref-Manual, p.586)</a> Indexing and access controls for surveillance records are described in manual references, illustrating how the FBI tracks and authorizes access to sensitive information. <a href="/pdf/FBI-FOIA-PA-Ref-Manual#page=79" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #FBI-FOIA-PA-Ref-Manual, p.79)</a>

### Legal and governance framework

#### Attorney General guidelines and statutory authority
A recurring theme is the Attorney General’s Guidelines for Domestic FBI Operations, which frame the permissible scope of domestic surveillance and the integration of national security and criminal authorities. <a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=120" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.120)</a> The documents also emphasize that surveillance methods must conform to applicable statutes, including the National Security Act and the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA), and related criminal provisions. <a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=119" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.119)</a><a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=120" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.120)</a><a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=122" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.122)</a>

#### Foreign intelligence and domestic operations
Several sources discuss boundary conditions between foreign intelligence activities and domestic FBI actions, highlighting the need to align surveillance with formal legal authorities while noting historical debates around political intelligence and civil liberties. <a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=56" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.56)</a><a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=119" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.119)</a> Interagency procedures and court-approved mechanisms are cited as necessary for legitimate surveillance both domestically and abroad. <a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=110" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.110)</a>

#### DoD coordination and cross-agency procedures
DoD manuals describe coordination requirements when surveillance involves U.S. persons abroad or DoD personnel, emphasizing FBI involvement and approvals as part of safeguarding process integrity. <a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=114" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.114)</a><a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=110" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.110)</a> DoD materials also discuss procedures to protect constitutional rights while enabling necessary intelligence collection. <a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=110" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.110)</a>

#### Privacy and civil liberties considerations
Privacy and civil liberties concerns are foregrounded in FOIA-derived texts, which discuss balancing security needs with individual rights and protecting Fourth Amendment rights amid surveillance capabilities. <a href="/pdf/NARAclintonPLfoiaLogs2008-2020#page=97" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #NARAclintonPLfoiaLogs2008-2020, p.97)</a> These concerns are contextualized within broader discussions of privacy in related materials. <a href="/pdf/FBItransBriefBiden_2020#page=12" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2020, p.12)</a> Additional historical discussion emphasizes balancing surveillance with constitutional protections. <a href="/pdf/FBI_In_Our_Open_Society_1953#page=17" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1953, p.17)</a>

### Historical context and notable programs

#### Early debates and civil liberties
Declassified discussions reflect ongoing debates about surveillance’s security benefits and civil liberties impacts. <a href="/pdf/FBI_In_Our_Open_Society_1953#page=17" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1953, p.17)</a> Modern scrutiny and reform discussions have followed in the wake of evolving capabilities. <a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=55" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.55)</a>

#### COINTELPRO and political intelligence
Sources refer to COINTELPRO-era activities and the broader historical context where domestic surveillance intersected with political intelligence, illustrating concerns about civil liberties and potential government overreach. <a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=56" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.56)</a>

#### Post-Church Committee reflections
Church Committee-era discussions contributed to growing awareness of surveillance practices and the evidence used to assess civil liberties impacts, underscoring reform and oversight that inform current frameworks. <a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=55" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.55)</a>

#### Technical surveillance countermeasures
Historical records from the 1960s and 1970s show oversight of technical surveillance countermeasures, including commissions and committees that analyzed covert techniques and the balance between legitimate intelligence collection and countermeasures. <a href="/documents/FBI-NSC-SCTSC_1963-1964_chunk24" class="citation" target="_blank">[FBI-NSC-SCTSC_1963-1964_chunk24]</a>

#### Notable program governance
Overviews document routine reviews by program coordinators and the role of oversight to maintain accountability for investigative activities, including after-action evaluations and interagency feedback. <a href="/pdf/FBI-AshReport_1977-1981#page=246" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1977-1981, p.246)</a><a href="/pdf/9IC-IGclosDocsInvests_2012-2013#page=9" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2012-2013, p.9)</a>

### Modern considerations and challenges

#### Going dark and lawful access
Contemporary discussions focus on data access, encryption, and law enforcement’s ability to conduct surveillance amid expanding digital networks, framed as part of the Going Dark discourse and Lawful Access initiatives. <a href="/pdf/JMDpresidTransBriefs2000-2016#page=509" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #JMDpresidTransBriefs2000-2016, p.509)</a><a href="/pdf/NMBtransBriefBiden_2020#page=55" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2020, p.55)</a> The FBI pursues ongoing efforts to adapt investigative capabilities to technological changes. <a href="/pdf/JMDpresidTransBriefs2000-2016#page=509" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #JMDpresidTransBriefs2000-2016, p.509)</a>

#### Technological proliferation and data management
Analyses emphasize that the growth of digital networks and data availability pressures the FBI to innovate in data interception, storage, and analysis while preserving accountability and privacy protections. <a href="/pdf/NMBtransBriefBiden_2020#page=55" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2020, p.55)</a> Modern discussions address surveillance modernization and the need for interoperable systems. <a href="/pdf/NMBtransBriefBiden_2020#page=55" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2020, p.55)</a>

#### International and domestic surveillance interfaces
FOIA-based sources describe interfaces between domestic operations and international surveillance considerations, including coordination with foreign intelligence and compliance with U.S. law. <a href="/pdf/CorresOLC-Kavanaugh_2001-2006#page=805" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2001-2006, p.805)</a><a href="/pdf/USA-CID-Reg195-1ext#page=31" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #USA-CID-Reg195-1ext, p.31)</a>

### Interagency coordination and governance

#### FBI, CIA, and cross-agency collaboration
Investigation and oversight materials describe interactions among the FBI, CIA, and other agencies when surveillance actions involve shared interests. <a href="/pdf/9IC-IGclosDocsInvests_2012-2013#page=9" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2012-2013, p.9)</a><a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=110" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.110)</a> The aim is to avoid duplicative or conflicting actions while preserving legal compliance. <a href="/pdf/9IC-IGclosDocsInvests_2012-2013#page=9" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2012-2013, p.9)</a>

#### DoD and civilian law enforcement coordination
DoD materials reiterate that physical surveillance outside the United States typically requires coordination with the FBI and other law enforcement agencies. <a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=114" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.114)</a><a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=110" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.110)</a>

### Practical implications and records management

#### Data storage and access controls
FOIA-derived statements emphasize centralized storage of investigative data at FBI Headquarters and controlled access through official channels. <a href="/pdf/FBI-AshReport_1977-1981#page=246" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1977-1981, p.246)</a> Indexing and access controls for surveillance records are described in manual references, illustrating how the FBI tracks and authorizes access to sensitive information. <a href="/pdf/FBI-FOIA-PA-Ref-Manual#page=586" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #FBI-FOIA-PA-Ref-Manual, p.586)</a><a href="/pdf/FBI-FOIA-PA-Ref-Manual#page=79" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #FBI-FOIA-PA-Ref-Manual, p.79)</a>

#### Recordkeeping and accountability
Documents note routine review processes and the role of program coordinators in overseeing investigative activities. <a href="/pdf/FBI-AshReport_1977-1981#page=246" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1977-1981, p.246)</a><a href="/pdf/FBI-AshReport_1977-1981#page=155" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1977-1981, p.155)</a> After-action reports and interagency evaluations further contribute to policy learning and refinement. <a href="/pdf/9IC-IGclosDocsInvests_2012-2013#page=9" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2012-2013, p.9)</a>

### Key findings and synthesis
- Surveillance in FBI practice spans electronic and physical modalities, each subject to legal authorization and interagency coordination. <a href="/pdf/FBI-AshReport_1977-1981#page=246" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1977-1981, p.246)</a><a href="/pdf/9IC-IGclosDocsInvests_2012-2013#page=9" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2012-2013, p.9)</a> 
- Data generated by investigations are centralized at FBI Headquarters and governed by strict access controls within the Central Records System. <a href="/pdf/FBI-AshReport_1977-1981#page=246" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #1977-1981, p.246)</a>
- The legal architecture shaping domestic FBI operations includes Attorney General guidelines and statutes such as FISA and the criminal code, with an emphasis on ensuring compliance and protecting civil liberties. <a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=119" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.119)</a><a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=120" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.120)</a>
- Historical debates around surveillance—from early privacy concerns to COINTELPRO and Church Committee investigations—inform contemporary oversight and reform initiatives. <a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=55" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.55)</a><a href="/pdf/ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009#page=56" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #ThesisRegDomIntelSurvAct2009, p.56)</a>
- Modern challenges center on technological change, data access, and going dark concerns, prompting FBI strategies described in recent briefings on lawful access and technology adaptation. <a href="/pdf/JMDpresidTransBriefs2000-2016#page=509" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #JMDpresidTransBriefs2000-2016, p.509)</a><a href="/pdf/FBItransBriefBiden_2020#page=12" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2020, p.12)</a>
- DoD–FBI coordination for operations involving U.S. persons abroad or covert surveillance is framed to respect constitutional rights while enabling necessary intelligence gathering. <a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=110" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.110)</a><a href="/pdf/DIA_FileId_238597#page=114" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #238597, p.114)</a>
- UAVs and other modern surveillance modalities are explicitly governed by a mix of constitutional protections, privacy laws, and internal FBI regulations, illustrating the layered regulatory environment now governing field operations. <a href="/pdf/FBIcorresHJC_2012-2013#page=70" class="citation" target="_blank">(Doc #2012-2013, p.70)</a>
